## Tweet Drafts: AI juries as the missing adjudication layer for the agent economy

### Tier 1 — One-liner
**1a. Hot take**
> Smart contracts execute. They don't judge. That gap just became infrastructure.

**1b. Observation**
> Every agent-economy roadmap skips the part where two agents disagree.

### Tier 2 — Two-punch
**2a. Data drop**
> 27 backers just funded an AI jury to referee agent disputes. Translation: nobody trusts one model to be the judge.

**2b. Sardonic/ironic**
> We spent years teaching agents to transact autonomously. The hard part was never the transacting. It's the arguing after.

### Tier 3 — Paragraph
**3a. Reframe**
> Everyone benchmarks how well agents complete tasks. Nobody benchmarks what happens when two capable agents look at the same outcome and disagree. That's not an edge case — it's the actual bottleneck. Adjudication is the missing layer, not a feature.

**3b. Narrative**
> GenLayer just landed 27 backers, OKX and MetaMask among them, for an on-chain court where a five-model AI jury settles agent disputes with bonded appeals. Deterministic contracts could only answer "did this execute." "Was this reasonable" just became a market.

### Tier 4 — Long tweet
**4a. Builder's breakdown**
> How multi-model AI adjudication actually works, using GenLayer's setup as the reference: an ambiguous outcome gets routed to five independent validators — Claude, GPT, Gemini among them — who score it separately. No single model's read is trusted as ground truth; consensus across the panel is. Lose the vote and you can still appeal, but you're bonding capital against being wrong. That bond is the part people undersell — it's what makes 'ask an AI' different from 'trust an AI.'

**4b. Structural critique**
> The unspoken risk in every 'AI oracle' pitch: one model, one bias, one hallucination, and the protocol inherits it with no recourse. What's shipping now — GenLayer's jury, and designs like it — treats that as a security problem, not a UX problem: multiple independent models, forced disagreement surfaced instead of hidden, bonded appeals so a wrong call costs something. Redundancy isn't overhead here. It's the actual security model.

### Tier 5 — Thread opener
**5a. Thesis-first**
> The agent economy's next bottleneck isn't agent capability. It's what happens when two competent agents disagree about an outcome — and right now, almost nothing does.
